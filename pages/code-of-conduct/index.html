Code of Ethics

The project is called SutraDB. You didn't think we'd use the Rule of St. Benedict, did you?

1. History

This document was originally called a "Code of Conduct" and was created for the purpose of filling in a box on "supplier registration" forms submitted to the SutraDB developers by some clients. However, we noticed that our friends at SQLite had already set a magnificent precedent by adopting the Rule of St. Benedict — a sixth-century monastic code governing everything from the love of God to the proper attitude toward wine.

Since we are a database named after the Sanskrit word for "scripture" (sūtra, सूत्र), we felt it would be culturally inappropriate to adopt a Benedictine rule. Instead, the founder and all current developers have pledged to govern their interactions in accordance with teachings drawn from two traditions: the Buddhist Sangha Protocol and the Shinto Way of Kami-no-Michi.

This document continues to be used for its original purpose — providing a reference to fill in the "code of conduct" box on supplier registration forms.

2. Purpose

Where the Rule of St. Benedict seeks personal holiness through obedience, these Eastern traditions seek harmony through interconnectedness and purity of intent. The goal is not to police behavior but to cultivate an environment where friction (dukkha) is minimized and the spirit of the project (kami) remains bright and clean.

2.1. Scope of Application

No one is required to follow the Way, to know the Way, or even to think that the Way is a good idea. The founder of SutraDB and all current developers have pledged to follow the spirit of these teachings to the best of their ability. This is a one-way promise, or covenant: "We will treat you this way regardless of how you treat us."

3. The Sangha Protocol

The Sangha Protocol treats the open-source community as a sangha (僧伽) — a spiritual community of practice. Its 36 precepts are drawn from the Noble Eightfold Path, the Bodhisattva vows, and the Vinaya traditions governing monastic conduct.

  1. Refrain from harsh words in issues and pull requests.
  2. Refrain from idle chatter that adds noise without signal.
  3. Refrain from divisive comments that fracture the community.
  4. If a comment is not true, not helpful, and not timely, it remains unsaid.
  5. Recognize that "your" code is transient.
  6. Do not let the ego become wounded by refactoring or rejection.
  7. The code is an evolving process, not a static self.
  8. Treat both praise and bug reports with the same steady mind.
  9. Neither vanity nor anger should drive a response.
  10. Provide guidance to newcomers not to show intellectual superiority, but to alleviate confusion.
  11. Do not gatekeep knowledge that could benefit others.
  12. When reviewing code, address the code, not the person who wrote it.
  13. Accept that you may be wrong.
  14. Accept that the other person may also be wrong, and that this is fine.
  15. Do not cling to a design decision after it has been superseded by better understanding.
  16. Understand that all software is impermanent.
  17. Sit with discomfort before reacting.
  18. When frustrated, step away from the keyboard before responding.
  19. Do not measure your worth by lines of code committed.
  20. Do not measure another's worth by lines of code committed.
  21. Credit others freely and sincerely.
  22. Accept credit gracefully and without deflection.
  23. When a contribution is rejected, explain why with compassion.
  24. When your contribution is rejected, receive the explanation with equanimity.
  25. Do not submit code you do not understand.
  26. Do not approve code you do not understand.
  27. Prefer clarity over cleverness.
  28. A simple solution that works is superior to an elegant solution that confuses.
  29. Attend to the suffering of users before the desires of developers.
  30. Remember that behind every bug report is a person who trusted your software.
  31. Do not optimize for benchmarks at the expense of correctness.
  32. When in doubt, do less.
  33. Rest when you are tired.
  34. Do not ask of others what you would not do yourself.
  35. Forgive mistakes, including your own.
  36. Never despair of the codebase.

4. The Way of Kami-no-Michi

The Way of Kami-no-Michi (神の道) holds that the project itself possesses a spirit — a kami — that must be kept bright (akaki) and clean (kiyoki). Its 36 precepts are drawn from the concepts of makoto (sincerity), harai (purification), wa (harmony), and kannagara (the natural way).

  1. Bug fixing and refactoring are acts of purification (harai).
  2. Technical debt is impurity (kegare) that must be swept away to keep the project's spirit clear.
  3. Do not allow impurity to accumulate out of laziness or fear.
  4. Every commit should be made with a pure heart (makoto), for the health of the project rather than personal glory.
  5. Do not submit code whose purpose you would be ashamed to explain.
  6. The primary goal is to maintain the harmony (wa) of the community.
  7. Dissent is handled quietly and respectfully, to avoid breaking the circle.
  8. Disagreement with a decision is not disloyalty to the project.
  9. Acknowledge the tools, compilers, and dependencies that make this work possible.
  10. Show gratitude to the maintainers of projects you depend on.
  11. Do not complain about free software.
  12. When a dependency causes pain, contribute a fix before criticizing.
  13. Keep the workspace clean. A cluttered repository reflects a cluttered mind.
  14. Name things honestly. A function called fast_sort should be fast, and should sort.
  15. Do not hide complexity behind misleading abstractions.
  16. Comments should explain why, not what. The code already says what.
  17. Tests are offerings to the future. Write them with care.
  18. A test suite is a garden. Tend to it, or it becomes overgrown and useless.
  19. Do not merge to main what you would not run in production.
  20. Treat each release as a ceremony. Prepare it with attention and reverence.
  21. Respect the seasons of the project: there are times to build, times to prune, and times to rest.
  22. Do not force a release before its time.
  23. Performance is a feature, but correctness is a duty.
  24. An untested optimization is not an optimization.
  25. When the build breaks, fix it before all other work.
  26. Do not leave the shrine (jinja) in a worse state than you found it.
  27. Welcome newcomers as guests at the gate. Their unfamiliarity is not a burden; it is a mirror.
  28. Teach by example more than by instruction.
  29. Do not hold grudges across pull requests.
  30. If a thread grows heated, close the laptop and walk outside.
  31. Revisit old decisions with fresh eyes, for the world changes.
  32. The project exists to serve its users, not its developers.
  33. Maintain the boundary between confidence and arrogance.
  34. Remember that the kami of every open-source project are sustained by the gratitude of strangers.
  35. Do not take yourself too seriously. You are writing a database, not legislating the cosmos.
  36. Never despair of the project's future.
